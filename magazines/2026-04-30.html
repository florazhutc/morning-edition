Zed 1.0: The Native Editor Built to Surpass Electron's Performance Ceiling

To create a fundamentally better editor, we had to invent a new approach to building desktop software. Our previous editor, Atom , was built as a fork of Chromium, spawning the Electron framework in the process. Electron eventually became the foundation of VS Code (which today seems to be forked into a new AI code editor every other week). Web technology offered an easy path to shipping flexible software, but it also imposed a ceiling. No matter how hard we worked, we couldn't make Atom better than the platform it was built on.

Actionable Insight

Zed 1.0 critiques the prevalent Electron-based editor model, specifically its performance ceiling, despite its rapid development benefits. By promising a "fundamentally better approach to building desktop software," Zed positions itself as a challenger seeking to deliver superior native performance and a more integrated user experience, potentially influencing the next wave of developer tools. This move highlights an industry desire to push beyond the compromises of web technologies for demanding applications.

Community Voice

The Hacker News community largely praises Zed 1.0 for its speed, innovative technology, and integrated features, with many finding it a "sticky" and compelling daily driver. However, significant concerns were raised regarding its slow TypeScript LSP performance, compatibility issues with legacy project codebases, and certain UI/UX choices like the search tab behavior. Additionally, a specific clause in the license agreement about customer data use has caused apprehension for some potential users.

🛡️ Software Security

Rust's Safety Features Miss 44 CVEs in Core Utilities

Bugs Rust Won't Catch | corrode Rust Consulting In April 2026, Canonical disclosed 44 CVEs in uutils, the Rust reimplementation of GNU coreutils that ships by default since 25.10. Most of them came out of an external audit commissioned ahead of the 26.04 LTS. I read through the list and thought there’s a lot to learn from it. What’s notable is that all of these bugs landed in a production Rust codebase, written by people who knew what they were doing, and none of them were caught by the borrow checker, clippy lints , or cargo audit .

Actionable Insight

This revelation of 44 CVEs in a production Rust codebase, uncaught by its celebrated safety features, highlights that even advanced type systems and static analysis have blind spots. These bugs often stem from logical flaws, incorrect specifications, or design errors that reside at a higher abstraction level than what the borrow checker or lints can enforce. Consequently, thorough external audits, rigorous testing, and robust secure development practices remain indispensable, irrespective of the programming language.

Community Voice

The community consensus is that the reported bugs are not a failing of Rust itself, but highlight developers' lack of deep understanding of intricate Unix system APIs, their pitfalls (like TOCTOU race conditions), and the hidden historical lessons embedded in long-standing projects like Coreutils. There's also significant criticism directed at the project's amateur execution and the decision-making process at Ubuntu for incorporating it into a production environment.
